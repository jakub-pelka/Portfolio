import { ProcessBlock, AboutSection, TakeawaysSection } from "./components";

<AboutSection>
During my theater internship I saw first-hand what prop management looks like in practice —
and how far it is from any kind of system. Before writing a single line of code,
I checked what the market offered. Warehouse systems exist, but they assume things
that simply don't hold in theater.
</AboutSection>

<ProcessBlock type="problem" title="Inventory chaos in a theater" index={1}>
Theater props have no inventory system beyond the memory of the people who work with them.

- Multiple rooms, multiple piles — no labeled shelves
- Props disappeared even from labeled boxes designated for specific shows
- The only record: memory and phone photos

The need was real — but the solution had to fit the people and the place, not the other way around.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="decision" title="Market analysis: ready-made tools don't fit" index={2}>
Before writing a line of code, I checked what was available.

- WMS systems require scanners, licenses, and labeled shelf locations
- In a theater there are no shelves — there are piles in the corners of rooms
- Prop managers are not technical users; learning WMS software is a barrier, not a convenience

The problem wasn't price or availability — it was false assumptions about what the work actually looks like.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="decision" title="Hierarchy instead of location" index={3}>
Instead of precise location (shelf B3, row 2) I built a grouping system: room → group → item.

- Prop managers only need to know something "is somewhere in the main props room"
- Groups became more important than individual items — they organize the "warehouse"
- Adding an item to a group is enough; everything else follows from the hierarchy

Precision isn't always a value — sometimes orientation is enough.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="insight" title="Show notepad: feature came from the users" index={4}>
Prop managers needed a place to write what to prepare before a show.

- What to pull out and from where
- Who to tell something or hand something off to
- What to add, fix, or check before going onstage

The notepad with interactive checklists (Tiptap) didn't come from analysis — it came from a conversation.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="decision" title="AI-assisted cataloging: human-in-the-loop" index={5}>
Prop managers walk around photographing items — Gemini generates a name and description, a human approves.

- No description enters the database without a human review
- Initial cataloging of dozens of items: from hours to minutes
- AI as an accelerator, not a decision-maker

One photo and a few seconds instead of manually typing every record.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="problem" title="Three features that sounded great" index={6}>
Live checklist, room mapping, and vector search — I cut all three.

- Live checklist interrupted the workflow instead of helping; prop managers went back to paper
- Room mapping was redundant — rooms are fixed and everyone knows them
- Vector search sounds impressive, but prop managers search for specific things, not "similar" ones

A painful decision — but the right one.
</ProcessBlock>

<ProcessBlock type="milestone" title="Outcome: working product, not adopted" index={7}>
The app works and was tested in a theater environment — but it never entered daily use.

- The theater lacked resources for rollout and habit change
- Project rebuilt for portfolio presentation

Not every project makes it to production. This one taught me more than many that did.
</ProcessBlock>

<TakeawaysSection>
I came away convinced that a well-chosen feature does more than ten impressive ones.
The checklist notepad came out of a conversation, not analysis — and it turned out
more important than vector search that looked great on paper.

This project didn't come from a market gap. It came from watching specific people do specific work.
</TakeawaysSection>
